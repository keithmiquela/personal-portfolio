Learning Front-End Development

As a Computer Science student at Rutgers University in 2025, I have been surrounded by the fear of impending doom of the job market that waits for me in 2028. Juggling through many potential options to target for my career, there seems to be a common trend where projects are incredibly helpful to build experience. As I have seen, many "entry level" positions require a solid portfolio of work to even be considered, let alone years of experience.

I have tried to create projects throughout my freshman year of college and even attempting to go through freeCodeCamp's front-end developer course just to get the certificate. That is when I bumped into the issue that thought I was very good at computer science and math, I was not so good at software engineering. For the summer of 2025, as I remain internship-less and unexperienced, I have decided to focus on building my skills through personal projects and online courses. By taking the opportunity to go through the grueling and slow process of enforcing the fundamentals of full stack development, I will be able to show whatever project I choose to create contained in an attractive and operating website.

Through out the summer, I will focus on front-end development, covering essential topics such as HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. These technologies form the backbone of web development and are crucial for creating visually appealing and interactive user interfaces. These will lay the foundations of web design and web development to later be fully operational when connected to a backend. As I progress through this journey, I will document my journey through the resources and tips I have gained on the way.

Note Before Reading This

This is the summer of 2025. AI is slowly being integrating into software exngineering and web development. Though if you are in the same position as I was, you may rather skip the fundamentals and go straight to building projects by using AI tools. I have taken it upon myself to learn the fundamentals of web development before I start using AI tools to build projects. I believe that understanding the core concepts will make me a better developer in the long run. Also as I have experienced before, not understanding the fundamentals of web development will lead to a lot of confusion and frustration when trying to build projects in the long run.

Getting Started with HTML

For this part of the journey and how I imagine I will continue on for both learning CSS and JavaScript, I will be using the freeCodeCamp full stack developer course. One of the mistakes that I have made before is focusing on the end goal and getting the certificate rather than actually learning the material. I have decided to take a different approach this time. As this is the third time I am attempting at going through this curriculum, there are some concepts that I am already familiar with. However, to really enforce these concepts, I will be taking their advice and really going through the content in detail and breaking it into parts throughout the duration of the summer. I do have the time, so I do not find it necessarily to only limit my learning to 30 minutes a day, but what I will do is decide to stop when I am feeling tired or overwhelmed to avoid burnout. I will also be taking breaks in between to let the information sink in. Also, consistency is key, so I will be trying to get in at least one session a day.

After Completing the HTML Section

I have a solid understanding of HTML and its elements. I can create well-structured web pages using semantic HTML tags, and I am familiar with best practices for accessibility and SEO. In all honesty, some of the lectures were hard and long to get through but ultimately, I believe they were worth it. One of the things I would say is to start practicing immediately. Even though you may want to start using React Libraries or AI to create a website, I think there is something fundamental about creating an inherently terrible website as you learn and build on the website as you continue your journey.
